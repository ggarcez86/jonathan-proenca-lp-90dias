# Objection Sequences

For archetype B (Objection-Led) pages, the objection handling section is the conversion engine. This file maps canonical objections per niche and provides sequencing logic.

## Why objection sequencing matters

Cold traffic arrives with skepticism. The page either surfaces that skepticism and handles it, or the lead leaves silently. Silent exits are invisible conversion killers — they don't show up as drop-offs in analytics, they just don't convert.

Good objection handling:
- Names the objection the lead is already feeling
- Validates it (doesn't mock or dismiss)
- Reframes it in a way that creates a path forward
- Connects the reframe to the offer

## Canonical objections by niche

### Executive career mentorship / career advancement

**Objection 1 — Time**
- "I don't have time for another workshop / program"
- Reframe angle: This is exactly the problem — executives who don't promote are often trapped in tactical work. The workshop addresses the root of that trap. Length is 90 minutes.

**Objection 2 — Past failures**
- "I've tried coaching / courses / books before and nothing worked"
- Reframe angle: Most career content teaches generic frameworks (SMART goals, elevator pitches). This methodology is specific to the politics and pattern recognition of senior promotion, which generic content misses.

**Objection 3 — My situation is different**
- "My industry / company / role is unique — generic advice doesn't apply"
- Reframe angle: The method works at the level of organizational dynamics (how decisions about promotion actually get made), which is universal across sectors. Name 2–3 diverse sectors where clients have succeeded.

**Objection 4 — I can figure this out on my own**
- "I'm smart, I've gotten this far, I should be able to solve this"
- Reframe angle: The reason smart people plateau is that the rules change at senior levels — from "deliver results" to "be seen delivering results in a way decision-makers understand". You can't see your own blind spots. That's what the method addresses.

**Objection 5 — Is this really free / what's the catch**
- Implied at premium workshops — audience assumes there's a pitch
- Reframe angle: Be honest. "At the end of the workshop, you'll have the full method and three pathways. One of those is continuing with me in a paid program. No pressure, no hidden cost to attend."

### AI-powered methodology (sub-objection for this specific project)

**Objection 6 — AI can't really help with something as human as career politics**
- Reframe angle: AI doesn't replace human judgment — it accelerates pattern recognition across thousands of cases. The workshop shows how specifically to use it for career intelligence that a human coach couldn't surface alone.

**Objection 7 — I don't want to rely on AI / I don't trust AI output**
- Reframe angle: The method uses AI as a research and feedback tool — decisions are yours. You'll see exactly how to maintain control while leveraging capability.

## Sequencing logic

Not all objections belong on every page. Select 3–4 that match the specific audience and lead with the strongest.

**Sequence pattern for executive workshop:**

1. **Start with Time or Past Failures** — these are the most common and handling them early unblocks the rest of the page
2. **Then "My situation is different"** — this is the intellectual objection that keeps skeptical leads from engaging
3. **Then "I can figure this out"** — this is the ego objection; handling it primes them for the method reveal
4. **End with the AI-specific objection** — this is unique to the expert's positioning and transitions into the method preview

## Objection section structure (per objection)

Each objection gets one compact section. Template:

```
Heading: The objection, stated directly (in quotation marks or as a question)
Example: "Eu já tentei vários cursos de carreira e não funcionou."

Body paragraph (2–4 sentences):
- Acknowledge the objection is legitimate
- Explain WHY the previous attempts failed (structurally, not personally)
- Bridge to why this is different
```

## Copy tone rules

- **Validate first.** Never start with "But actually..." — start with "Makes sense that you'd feel this way, because..."
- **Use the audience's language.** If the audience says "me promoverem" (passive), use "me promoverem" in the objection statement. Don't sanitize it.
- **Specificity over generality.** "Generic career advice fails because it treats promotion as a meritocracy when it's actually a perception game at senior levels" beats "Generic career advice fails because it's generic".
- **No sales-speak in objection sections.** This is where trust gets built. Save the pitch for the method reveal and final CTA.

## Red flags — objection handling done wrong

- ❌ Using a straw-man objection the audience isn't actually thinking ("You might think success requires a Harvard MBA — but...")
- ❌ Bullet lists of objections — these read as marketing, not conversation
- ❌ Using fear to handle objections ("If you don't act now, you'll still be in the same role in 5 years")
- ❌ Handling 7+ objections — the lead stops reading

## Alternative: the single-counter-narrative approach

Instead of handling individual objections, some premium pages use ONE counter-narrative section:

"If you've tried career coaching, productivity systems, and professional development programs and still haven't advanced, it's because nearly all of them solve the wrong problem. They teach tactics — communication frameworks, goal-setting templates, elevator pitches. Senior promotion isn't a tactics problem. It's a perception problem. This workshop is about the perception layer that tactical programs can't reach."

This works better for:
- Editorial/luxury clean aesthetics
- Shorter pages (archetype C)
- Expert brands strong enough to command assertion

Use when the rhythm of multiple objection sections would disrupt the premium tone.
