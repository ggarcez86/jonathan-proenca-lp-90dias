# Page Architectures

Three structural archetypes for capture pages. Each has a clear use case, section order, and trade-offs. Pick ONE based on offer type and traffic temperature.

---

## Archetype A — The Direct Funnel

**Use when:**
- Traffic is warm (email list, referrals, past customers)
- Offer is simple and self-explanatory (free workshop with clear topic)
- Expert brand is strong enough that extensive trust-building isn't needed
- Goal is maximum volume with minimal friction

**Section order:**

1. **Hero (full viewport)**
   - Pre-headline: offer type + date (e.g., "WORKSHOP ONLINE · 18 DE NOVEMBRO")
   - Headline: the promise
   - Subhead: who it's for + what they'll leave with
   - Form: right side or below headline, visible above fold
   - Light proof: one line ("300+ executivos já passaram pelo método")

2. **What you'll learn (3 bullets max)**
   - Three outcome-focused bullets, not features
   - Written as transformations, not topics

3. **Who this is for (short qualification list)**
   - 3–4 bullets describing the ideal attendee
   - Helps self-selection

4. **Expert credential block (short)**
   - Photo of expert
   - 2–3 sentence credential paragraph
   - 1 key number or named outcome

5. **Final CTA block**
   - Reinforces the offer
   - Form repeats OR CTA scrolls to hero form
   - Small urgency signal (date, registration closes)

**Total page length:** 3–4 viewport heights on desktop.

**Friction budget:** 5–8 points.

**When this fails:** For cold traffic or high-ticket offers, this archetype under-sells and converts poorly.

---

## Archetype B — The Objection-Led Page

**Use when:**
- Traffic is cold (paid ads, unknown audience)
- Offer requires mental commitment (attending a 90-minute workshop)
- Audience has prior scar tissue from similar offers
- Expert brand is less established

**Section order:**

1. **Hero**
   - Pre-headline: event type + date
   - Headline: the promise + specificity
   - Subhead: who it's for (self-qualifying)
   - Form: above fold, right side or below headline
   - Micro-proof: one line or small logo strip

2. **The problem statement (1 section)**
   - Paints the current pain the audience feels
   - Written in the audience's own language
   - Short — this is not a sales page

3. **Why it's not their fault (1 section)**
   - Reframes the problem as systemic, not personal
   - Introduces the premise of the expert's method
   - Transitions toward authority

4. **Expert authority block**
   - Photo + credentials
   - Specific numbers (years of experience, clients served, named companies)
   - Connects their background to why they can solve THIS problem

5. **Objection handling — 2 to 4 objections**
   - Each objection as its own short section
   - Pattern: "You might be thinking [objection] — here's why that's not the case in this method"
   - Common objections for executive niche: time, past failures, "my situation is different", "I can figure this out"

6. **Method preview**
   - What the workshop will cover, as transformation bullets
   - Not a curriculum — an outcome list

7. **Social proof block (heavy)**
   - 1 featured video testimonial OR 2–3 detailed written testimonials with specifics
   - Names, roles, companies where possible

8. **FAQ (short)**
   - 3–5 questions that handle remaining friction
   - Questions real prospects ask, not invented ones

9. **Final CTA block**
   - Urgency reminder (date, registration closes)
   - Form repeats OR scroll-to-form CTA
   - Reassurance micro-copy below CTA

**Total page length:** 6–8 viewport heights on desktop.

**Friction budget:** 8–12 points. Higher friction is acceptable because the content does the heavy lifting of pre-qualification.

**When this fails:** For warm audiences who already trust the expert, this is overkill and adds friction without benefit.

## Archetype C — The Authority-Led Page

**Use when:**
- Expert is a known name (book author, media personality, senior figure)
- Offer is selective (application-based mentorship, small cohort)
- Positioning is premium / old money / exclusive
- Goal is lead quality over volume

**Section order:**

1. **Hero — minimal, editorial**
   - No traditional pre-headline
   - Single oversized phrase that signals the domain
   - Small line below with date + context
   - No form above fold — button that says "Request invitation" or "Apply for consideration" leading to form below

2. **Expert introduction (long-form)**
   - Editorial paragraph introducing the expert
   - Portrait-style photograph, high quality
   - Mentions of books, media, companies they've led — natural name-drops

3. **What this session is (not a feature list)**
   - Prose description of the workshop, written as invitation
   - Specific audience it's designed for
   - Conversational, not bullet-pointed

4. **Method essence (1 section)**
   - The core insight or framework, named
   - Gives away enough to demonstrate depth
   - Premium positioning: show you have more than enough

5. **Previous attendees (name-heavy proof)**
   - Companies and roles listed (not necessarily testimonial quotes)
   - The credibility is in the roster
   - If testimonials used: 1 long-form quote from a recognizable name

6. **The form — framed as RSVP/application**
   - Framed as "Request your invitation" or "Apply for the session"
   - Form has a qualifier that signals selectivity
   - Copy acknowledges the curated nature

7. **Closing note from the expert**
   - Personal sign-off
   - Signature or handwritten-style element
   - Contact for questions (human, not a form)

**Total page length:** 4–6 viewport heights on desktop.

**Friction budget:** 10–14 points. Friction is a feature — it signals selectivity.

**When this fails:** If the expert isn't actually well-known, this archetype feels pretentious and under-converts. Requires real authority.

## Which archetype for Gustavo's executive mentorship project?

Based on memory: the expert's methodology helps professionals advance in their careers and uses AI heavily. Likely scenario is paid traffic to a workshop that pitches the mentorship.

**Primary recommendation: Archetype B (Objection-Led)**, because:
- Paid traffic is cold-ish (even if well-targeted)
- "Get promoted" category has significant scar tissue (people have tried career coaching, productivity systems)
- The AI-powered angle needs explanation, not just a headline claim
- Workshop → pitch flow benefits from pre-selling before the workshop

**Secondary option: Archetype C** if the expert has significant personal brand (book, media presence, C-level at named companies). If yes, the authority-led version converts better qualified leads.

**Not recommended: Archetype A**, unless running to a warm email list only.

## Mobile considerations

On mobile, ALL archetypes should:
- Move form to its own position below hero (not crammed into hero split)
- Hero CTA button scrolls to form
- Every section collapses to stacked vertical
- Testimonial grids become one-per-viewport
- FAQ accordions collapsed by default
- Final CTA section appears one viewport before footer (don't bury it)

For archetype B specifically, consider a sticky bottom CTA bar on mobile ("Registrar agora · Vagas abertas") that appears after user scrolls past hero.

---

## Section order decision checklist

For any archetype:
1. Does each section REDUCE a specific objection OR BUILD a specific trust component?
2. Is the form accessible (visible or reachable via CTA) from every viewport on the page?
3. Is the strongest proof at mid-page (not at top, not at bottom)?
4. Does the final CTA block give a reason to act NOW (real deadline or spot scarcity)?
5. Is anything on the page that, if removed, would HURT conversion? If no → cut.
