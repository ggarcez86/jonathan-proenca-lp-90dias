# Social Proof Strategy

Social proof is the second most important conversion element after the offer itself. This file maps which proof type to use, where, and why.

## The three proof zones

A capture page has three placements where proof should appear. Each zone has a different job.

### Zone 1 — Above fold (light proof)

**Job:** Reduce the "is this legit?" friction in the first 5 seconds.

**Format:** One line OR one logo strip. Never a testimonial block.

**Options:**
- Numbers line: "500+ executivos de empresas como [Company], [Company] e [Company]"
- Logo strip: 4–6 client logos, grayscale or low-contrast, no heading
- Credential line: "Método aplicado em [named list of recognizable companies]"
- Media line: "Apresentado em [Media outlet], [Media outlet]"

**Rules:**
- Must load fast (no testimonial videos or heavy images)
- Must be scannable in under 2 seconds
- Must be specific (numbers, names — not "trusted by thousands")

### Zone 2 — Mid-page (heavy proof)

**Job:** Convert the "interested but not convinced" lead into a committed one.

**Format:** ONE featured testimonial (video or long-form written) OR 2–3 outcome-focused written quotes.

**Options:**
- **Video testimonial** (strongest): 60–120 second talking-head from a recognizable client, with transcript available
- **Outcome-focused quote set**: 2–3 quotes where each includes a specific result ("Got promoted to VP within 7 months"), not generic praise
- **Case study snippet**: A short narrative — "Before the method, [client] was [specific situation]. After: [specific result]. Here's what shifted: [one-sentence mechanism]."

**Rules:**
- Names and roles REQUIRED (no "J.M., Manager")
- Specificity REQUIRED (no "it was life-changing")
- Results REQUIRED (no "I learned so much")
- If possible, company names (with permission)

### Zone 3 — Near final CTA (reassurance proof)

**Job:** Handle last-second doubt. Remind the lead they're not alone.

**Format:** A name-heavy bar, OR a second compact testimonial, OR a numbers reinforcement.

**Options:**
- "CFOs of [Company], CTOs of [Company], and Directors of [Company] have attended"
- "Workshop com 500+ inscritos em edições anteriores"
- A single short testimonial with photo, placed directly above the form

**Rules:**
- Should reinforce the ONE thing the offer does, not expand the claim
- Brief — lead is close to conversion, don't delay them

## Proof type selection by audience

### For executive / C-level audiences

**What works:**
- Named companies (big recognizable employers)
- Specific roles of testimonial givers (VP, Director, CFO — not "professional")
- Outcomes tied to career moves (promotions, comp changes, board appointments)
- Understated tone (over-excited testimonials read as fake to this audience)

**What doesn't:**
- Generic "this changed my life" quotes
- Anonymous testimonials ("Executive at Fortune 500")
- Emoji-heavy social screenshots
- Results tied to soft metrics only ("I feel more confident")

### For aspirational mid-career audiences

**What works:**
- Relatable testimonial givers (mid-manager → VP transition stories)
- Concrete numbers (salary increases, team size changes, role titles)
- Before/after narrative structure
- Mix of B2B company names and mid-market companies

**What doesn't:**
- Only C-level testimonials (intimidating, feels out of reach)
- Corporate-speak — this audience responds to direct language

### For high-ticket / selective mentorship

**What works:**
- Fewer, higher-quality testimonials
- Video over text
- Results measured in deals/outcomes (not satisfaction)
- Name-heavy proof at the bottom

**What doesn't:**
- Walls of testimonials — feels like they're compensating
- Screenshot of WhatsApp messages — OK for BR creator niche, wrong for premium executive

## Anti-patterns

- ❌ "As seen on [logo of random media outlet they got a passing mention in]" — this is over-claiming
- ❌ Testimonial with stock photo as the "client photo" — leads can tell
- ❌ Testimonials without names ("J. Silva, São Paulo") — reads as fake
- ❌ Five-star review blocks (Amazon-style) for a premium mentorship — wrong category
- ❌ "Join thousands of satisfied customers" — vague, lazy, and untrusted
- ❌ Logo strip where half the logos are small tools the expert uses, not clients

## When the expert doesn't have strong testimonials yet

If the expert is new to the offer and has limited social proof:

**Do:**
- Lean on the expert's personal credentials (companies they worked at, roles they held, books/media appearances)
- Use testimonials from related work (corporate training, keynotes, previous roles)
- State honestly: "First cohort · Early participants" — premium audiences respect honesty about stage

**Don't:**
- Fabricate testimonials
- Inflate numbers
- Use testimonials from unrelated work that don't transfer credibility

## Collecting proof for future iterations

Embed proof collection into the workshop itself:
- End-of-workshop form with 3 questions: "What was the most useful insight?" / "What changed in your thinking?" / "Would you recommend this?"
- Permission to use responses as testimonials, with name + role + company
- Follow up 30 days later: "What happened in your career since the workshop?"

This creates the testimonial pipeline for the next cohort.

## Placement output template

When producing a placement plan:

```
Social proof placement plan:

Zone 1 (above fold):
  Type: [Logo strip / Numbers line / Credential line]
  Content: [exact text or asset list]

Zone 2 (mid-page, after objection handling):
  Type: [Video testimonial / Quote set / Case study snippet]
  Content: [names, roles, companies, quote excerpts, video asset reference]
  Placement: Between [X section] and [Y section]

Zone 3 (near final CTA):
  Type: [Name-heavy bar / Compact quote / Numbers]
  Content: [exact text]

Total proof elements: [count]
Proof-to-claim ratio: [is every major claim backed by at least one proof element?]
```
